Queen's Gambit Declined: Exchange Variation

1.e4 e6 "the exchange variation"

2.d4 d5 3.exd5

the exchange variation was played actively for a while by Garry Kasparov himself, but it cannot be dangerous for Black. It is obvious that White can create considerably more problems for his opponent only with the moves 3.Nc3, 3.Nd2 and 3.e5. still, Black must play accurately. For those chess-fans who wish to play always only for a win, I would like to tell you that according to the professional players — there are two positive results in chess — a win and a draw...

3...exd5 4.Nf3

This is the most solid and flexible move for White. It is also possible for him to play 4.c4, but in general it is not so advantageous for him to clarify his plan so early in the game. Black has at his disposal a very harmonious set-up — 4...Nf6 5.Nf3 (He can counter 5.Nc3 with 5...Bb4.) 5...Bb4+ 6.Nc3 0-0 7.Be2 dxc4. Now, White has numerous alternatives, but they all have certain drawbacks. If Black so wishes, he can ignore his additional possibilities and stick to the same plan. 8.0—0 (After 8.Bxc4, it is reasonable for him to trade immediately the light-squared bishops with 8...Re8+ 9.Be3 Be6 10.Bxe6 Rxe6 11.0-0 Nc6 12.Bg5 Bxc3 13.bxc3 Qd5 14.Bxf6 Rxf6, although White still maintains some pressure with 15.Qb3.) 8...Bg4 (The alternative for Black is — 8...Be6!?) 9.Bxc4 Nc6 10.Be3. White is more or less forced to enter that position after 4.c4. Having in mind that he has recaptured on c4 in two moves, Black should not have problems after the opening. For example: 10..Qd7 11.h3 Bh5 12.Be2 Rfe8= and the prospects of both sides are approximately equal. After 4.Nf3, Black has several possibilities. I will concentrate on the move 4...Bd6, because of two reasons. At first, the situation after 5.c4 dxc4 6.Bc4, may arise in the Queen's gambit accepted and it is always useful to know about similar transpositions between different openings. I have in mind the variation 1.d4 d5 2.c4 dxc4 3.e3 e5 4.Bc4 exd4 5.exd4 Bd6 6.Nf3. Secondly, I think this same move combines reliability and positional justification and avoids complete symmetry enabling Black to think about winning the game after all...

4...Bd6

The move 4...Bg4 was popular during the 90ies of the past century, but after Kasparov found the idea 5.h3 Bh5 6.Qe2+!, it became evident that Black would have problems in this variation. 6...Qe7 7.Be3 Nc6 8.Nc3 0-0-0 9.0-0-0± Kasparov Short, Tilburg 1991. Black tries sometimes to provoke complications with the move 4...Nc6, but he may have difficulties in the well-known variation after 5.Bb5 Bd6 6.c4 dxc4 7.d5 a6 8.Ba4 b5 9.dxc6 bxa4 10.0—0 Ne7 11.Qxa4. White does not risk anything, while Black must still make several very accurate moves. Of course, his most reliable resource here is the symmetrical move — 4...Nf6. 5.c4 The famous principle of asymmetry in the Exchange variation can be illustrated here with the move — 5.Bd3. I should inform my readers that according to this principle Black should not be in a hurry to develop his king's knight early, since if his counterpart goes to f3, then Black should better deploy his knight to e7 and vice versa...Of course, you should not accept all these paradoxical principles so seriously after all, but still to have them in mind will always be useful. For example, in the next game Black followed all these rules and he managed to outplay gradually his opponent and to prevail in the endgame. 5...Ne7 6.0—0 Nbc6 7. h3 Nb4 8.Be2 Bf5 9.Na3 a6 1O.b3 0-0 11.c3 Nbc6 12.Nc2 Ng6 13. exd5 Bd3 Bxd3 14.Qxd3 Qd7 15.Bd2 Nce7 16.Rfe1 Qf5 17.Qxf5 Nxf5 18.g3 f6 19.Re2 Rfe8 20.Rae1 Rxe2 21.Rxe2 Kf7 22.Kg2 h5 Gorbatov — Rychagov, Moscow 2008.

5...dxc4 6.Bxc4 Nf6 7.0-0 0-0 8.Nc3

It is interesting for White to try to seize the initiative immediately with 8.Ne5!? Black must react very precisely: 8...Nc6! This is the right move! (It is weaker for him to opt for 8... Nbd7?! 9.Bf4 Nb6 10.Bb3 Nfd5 11.Bg3 Be6 12.Nd2 Re8 13.Ne4 Qe7 14.Rc1± and White obtained an advantage in the game Tregubov Vitiugov, Sochi, 2009.) 9.Nxc6 (Now, he cannot play 9.Bf4, because of the routine reply 9...Nxe5 10.dxe5 Ng4 and White cannot protect his pawn.) 9...bxc6. Black's pawn-structure has been compromised a bit, but he is not worse. For example: 10.Nc3 Re8 11.Qf3 Rb8 12.h3 Be6, and the dynamical factors are in his favour.

8...Nc6

Starting at that moment, the opening has many things in common with the Chigorin defence. It is obvious that in this rather original and complicated opening, Black's situation is considered as acceptable, but things are not so simple.

9.h3

The seemingly active move 9.Bg5, strangely enough, is not dangerous for Black at all. 9... h6 (It is not so precise for him to play 9...Bg4, since White can counter that with 10.•Nd5, obtaining the two bishop advantage. 10..Be7 11.Nxe7+ Qxe7 12.h3 Bxf3 13.Qxf3 Qe4 14.Qxe4 Nxe4 15.Be3 Nd6 16.b3 with some edge for him, Lputian — Romanishin, Manila 1992.) 10.Bh4 Bg4. It looks like White's best here is the forced draw after 11.h3 Bxf3 12.Qxf3 Nxd4 13.Qxb7 Rb8 14.Qxa7 Ra8 15.Qb7 Rb8, Gurevich — Azmaiparashvili, Saint Vincent 2003.

9...h6 10.Re1

It is difficult to say, which is White's most useful move here. He has tried many different ideas, but Black has good counter chances everywhere. In principle, all that is quite logical. Both sides play solidly in the centre and neither side should have problems. White cannot harm his opponent with 10.a3 Bf5 11.b4 Ne4! 12.Bb2 Nxc3 13.Bxc3 Qf6= It would be too academic for him to opt for 10. Be3 a6 11.a4 Bf5 12.Nh4 Bh7 13.Bd3 Bxd3 14.Qxd3 Nb4 15.Qd1 Re8 16.Qf3 Bf8 17.Rad1 Nbd5 18.Nf5 Kh7 19.Bf4 c6 20.Be5 Re6 Balashov — Morozevich, Samara 1998. White has an interesting possibility here 10.Qc2, with the idea to prevent the natural development of his opponent's lightsquared bishop. Black can react cleverly against that with 10...a6!?, or he can play simpler — 10...Nb4 11.Qb1 c6!? (White obtains some targets to attack after 11...Be6 12.Bxe6 fxe6 13.Re1 Qd7 14.Bd2 Nbd5 15.Qd3 Rad8 16.Re2 Qf7 17.Rae1 Rfe8 18.Ne4 Tkachiev Sulava, Gonfreville 2006.) with the idea after 12.Re1, to respond with 12...Nbd5. It also deserves attention for Black to try 10...Na5 11.Bd3 Be6. 11.a3 b5 12.Bd3 Bb7 with a good counterplay.

10...Re8

This is the most natural response for him. He has alternatives too. The move 10...Bf5 was tested in a game against a computer by the famous expert of the French defence Alexander Morozevich. 11.d5 Ne7 12.Be3 a6 13. Bd4 Ng6 14.a4 Re8 15.Qb3 b6 16. Rxe8+ Nxe8 17.Re1 Nf6 Fritz — Morozevich, Frankfurt 2000. Now, White exerts some positional pressure. 15. Nh4 Kh8 16.Nf5 Re8 17.Nxd6 (17. b4!?) 17...Qxd6 18.Rd1 (He cannot play 18.Bxf7, in view of 18... Ne5!) 18...Be6 19.Bxe6 Rxe6 20. d5 Ne5 21.Qd4 Re8 22.Bf4 (22. Qa7!?) 22...c5 23.dxc6 Qxd4 24. Rxd4 Nxc6=. The opponents agreed to a draw, Korchnoi — Ivanchuk, Frankfurt 1998.

"Sicilian"

1 e4 c5 2 Nf3 d6 3 Bb5+ Bd7 4 Bxd7+ Nxd7

This is the poor cousin of 4 .. Qxd7. Al though the knight recapture develops a piece, in practice Black has favoured the more active posting of the knight on c6. On d7 the knight places no pressure on the centre, in particular on the important d4 square, and thus allows White a safe space advantage. The position often resembles a Hedgehog-style structure with Black solid but passive. Again White must be careful not to allow either the ... b7-b5 or ... d6-d5 breaks. I advocate playing in the same manner as we do after 4 ... Qxd7. there are two positive results in chess — a win and a draw...

5 0-0

I was initially attracted to 5 c4!?, but 5 ... Ne5! is rather annoying to deal with. This is a useful idea to remember, and to try and prevent. The point is that following 6 Nxe5 dxe5 Black has the d4-outpost and pressure down the d-file which is more important than the doubled e-pawns.

5 ... Ngf6 6 Qe2 e6 7 b3 Be7 8 Bb2 0-0 9 c4 a6 10 d4 cxd4 11 Nxd4

This is the main line of the 4 ... Nxd7 system and you should make sure you feel comfortable here and are au fait with the plans for both sides.
